Something is happening

This is Kevin Roose, tech columnist at the New York Times, writing last week:

"I follow AI adoption pretty closely, and I have never seen such a yawning inside/outside gap. People in SF are putting multi-agent claudeswarms in charge of their lives… people elsewhere are still trying to get approval to use Copilot in Teams."

— Kevin Roose, New York Times

And more pointedly:

"It's possible that restrictive IT policies have created a generation of knowledge workers who will never fully catch up."

— Kevin Roose, New York Times

There's a gap opening up. Between people who are building with AI every day — and everyone else.

Will has been on one side of that gap for over two years.

What this has looked like

I know what you've seen.

Evenings on the laptop. Weekends building something instead of being present. "Just one more thing" that turns into hours. Holly wanting attention while I'm in the terminal.

You've been patient. More patient than I've earned.

And I know "I'm building something important" sounds like every other excuse for not showing up. You've heard it before. From me, probably more than once.

So I'm not going to lead with what I've built. I'm going to start with what I'm asking — and why.

The drive

You know my brain. You've lived with it for years.

Growing up with ADHD, I learned that fixation can be destructive — or it can be directed. The same wiring that makes me disappear into something for hours is the wiring that lets me build things other people can't.

As I've grown up and matured, I've learned to use that fixation for useful means. Not perfectly. But better.

This isn't a phase. I've been obsessed with AI tools since ChatGPT launched in late 2022 — before it was trendy, before everyone had an opinion. I saw what was coming and started building.

The Park, Mission Control, the agent team — none of this was assigned. It was built in evenings, weekends, and margins because I couldn't not build it. That drive is the thing that's hard to fake. You can teach skills. You can't teach obsession.

The investment

I downloaded Claude Code the day it launched — I've been using it as long as anyone. Not catching up. On the frontier from day one.

That's nearly a year of daily use. Not watching tutorials, not reading about it. Actually building.

This isn't science fiction. It's what I've been building while you've been patient.

Why this matters

My job title is Product Manager. I earn £45,000 a year.

But what I actually do — directing AI to build things, shipping working software, running multi-agent systems — doesn't fit neatly into any job description.

Traditional PM jobs would pay more. But they'd mean:

  • Leaving my tools at the door
  • Managing delivery instead of building
  • Being interchangeable with any other PM

The thing that makes me 5-10x more productive? It wouldn't matter. I'd be a £70k PM doing the same work as everyone else.

That's like hiring a pilot to drive a bus. Technically possible, but a waste of what they can actually do.

The core asset

What I've built isn't just "skills on a CV." It's a working system:

  • A productivity stack that compounds (The Park, Mission Control, the agents)
  • The technical fluency to extend it in any direction
  • 2+ years of daily practice directing AI to ship real things

The question is finding the right way to deploy it. There are several paths:

Path 1: Fractional work

2-3 companies, 1-2 days each per week. Bring my system to each. Get paid for what I ship.

£74,000
3 days/week × £550 × 45 weeks/year

Path 2: Join an AI-native team

A small company (4-5 people) where AI leverage is the operating model. They exist — they're just not on traditional job boards. They'd value the stack, not ask me to abandon it.

Path 3: Build with a partner

Find someone who sees what this system can do and wants to build something together. A co-founder, a client who becomes a collaborator, an opportunity I haven't found yet.

The fractional route is the most concrete right now. But the core bet is on the stack and what it enables — not on any single path.

The plan

I'm not quitting my job tomorrow. Here's the actual path:

Now: Keep Alertacall

Stable income while building the next thing.

Next: Build visibility

Get willpalmer.co.uk live, update LinkedIn, start being visible in the AI/product space.

Then: Find 1-2 small clients

Fractional work on the side — maybe 1 day a week each.

Once there's traction

£2-3k/month coming in from outside → conversation with Alertacall about part-time.

End state

2-3 days/week billed to multiple clients. More money. Work on my terms. More present at home.

That's months away, not weeks. And it depends on things going right — finding clients, putting in the work on visibility, the market being receptive.

What this isn't:

  • It's not "quit and hope for the best"
  • It's not betting everything on one idea
  • It's not ignoring the financial reality

The £180/month question

The Claude subscription is the engine that makes all of this work.

I know it's a real expense. You're right to ask whether it's worth it.

Remember Kevin Roose's point about AI companies that "took scaling seriously, started stockpiling GPUs before 2022" having an insurmountable head start?

The £180 is my GPU stockpile.

It's the investment that keeps me on the frontier while others wait for corporate approval. Cut it, and I fall back into the pack of people who "will never fully catch up."

This isn't a Netflix subscription. It's the infrastructure that makes everything else possible.

The honest answer: the plan needs to show results by end of Q1, or it's fair to revisit.

What I'm asking

Space to execute this plan through the end of Q1. That's about 8 weeks.

The end state isn't more time on the computer. It's work on my terms — which means more time with you and Holly, not less.

I'm not asking for blind faith. I'm asking for:

  • Space to execute the plan through March
  • The £180/month investment to stay on the frontier
  • A check-in at end of Q1 — if it's not working, we reassess together

If it works, we're in a better position: more income, more flexibility, more presence.

If it doesn't, I'll have tried — and we'll figure out the next step together.

— Will
